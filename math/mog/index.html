The center of Germany

Posted on 2022, Oct 07

The Problem

A few days ago a friend told me that multiple cities claim to be the “center” of Germany. I was very confused, because for me the projection of the average point to germany was the obvious choice. My idea was, that such a “center” would minimize the integral over Germany of the distance between the center and points \(p\) of Germany. I was wrong. Even though I was wrong, I still think the problem was fun to think about. Let me guide you through my thought process.

Naive mathematical formulation

Let \((M,\varphi)\) be a Riemannian manifold of dimension \(k\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). I call a point \(p\in M\) central, if \[ \int_M \Vert p-x \Vert^2 d\mathcal{H}^k(x) = \inf_{p’\in M} \int_M \Vert p’-x \Vert^2 d\mathcal{H}^k(x). \]

Counter example 1: Not unique

Let \(M=B_1=\{x\in\mathbb{R}^2: \Vert x\Vert =1\}\). Now

\[ \int_M xd\mathcal{H}^1(x)=0 \]

The projection of the zero vector onto the boundary of the unit ball isn’t unique (by definition)!

Let’s see which points are central: Consider \(p\in M\) and let \[V(p)=\int_M \Vert p-x \Vert^2 d\mathcal{H}^1(x).\] Then \[V(p)=\int_M \Vert p-x \Vert^2 d\mathcal{H}^1(x) = 4\pi. \text{ (See Computation 1 for details)}\] Therefore all points on the boundary of the unit ball are central (but my candidate(s) was/were correct in this case).

Counter example 2: The projection of average is not necessarily central

My initial guess for potential central points was correct in the previous example. This is is not true in general!

Computation 1: Every point on \(B_1\) is central

Let \(p\in M\) be a point. Then \(p=(\cos(a),\sin(a))\) for some \(a\in [0,2\pi)\).

\[V(p)=\int_M \Vert p-x \Vert^2 d\mathcal{H}^1(x)=\int_0^{2\pi} (\cos(x)-\cos(a))^2+(\sin(x)-\sin(a))^2dx\]

We can split this integral into

\[ I_1=\int_0^{2\pi} (\cos(x)-\cos(a))^2+dx \]

and

\[ I_2=\int_0^{2\pi} (\sin(x)-\sin(a))^2+dx \]

After a few elementary computations we get \[I_1=\left[\frac{1}{2}(x+\sin(x)\cos(x))-2\cos(a)\sin(x)+x\cos^2(a)\right]_0^{2\pi}\]

and

\[I_2=\left[\frac{1}{2}(x-\sin(x)\cos(x))+2\sin(a)\cos(x)+x\sin^2(a)\right]_0^{2\pi}.\]

Therefore

\[I_1+I_2=\left[x - 2\cos(a)\sin(x)+2\sin(a)\cos(x) + x\left(\sin^2(x)+\cos^2(x)\right)\right]_0^{2\pi}=4\pi.\]
